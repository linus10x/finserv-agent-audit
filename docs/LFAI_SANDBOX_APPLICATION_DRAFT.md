## Application content (mapped to standard LFAI Sandbox template fields)

### Project name

`finserv-agent-audit`

### Project description (200 words)

`finserv-agent-audit` is an open-source governance framework for autonomous AI agents deployed in regulated US financial services. It provides reference implementations of six core governance patterns (autonomy-level classification A0-A4, sovereign veto, DEFCON risk-state, shadow-mode rollout, hash-chain audit logging with within-trust-boundary tamper-detection, and vendor scoring drift detection), six FSI-specific governance gates (model inventory for SR 11-7, adverse-action notification for FCRA and CFPB Circular 2022-03, suspicious-activity reporting workflow for BSA/AML, fair-lending pre-emit check for ECOA / Reg B, best-interest check for SEC Reg-BI, and a mutual-information protected-class-proxy detector), seven v1.3 modules addressing the discrimination frontier and third-party vendor surface, and v2.0 agentic-runtime adapters for Google A2A, LangGraph, Microsoft Agent Framework, and CrewAI. The framework ships a CycloneDX 1.7 ML-BOM + SPDX 3.0 AI Profile generator, a FastAPI governance endpoint with OpenAPI 3.1 spec, and a Kubernetes operator scaffold with three custom resource definitions. Maps to 20+ regulatory frameworks including SR 11-7, OCC 2011-12 (historical) + OCC 2026-13 overlay, EU AI Act, DORA, NIST AI RMF, NIST AI 600-1, ISO/IEC 42001, NYDFS Part 500, NAIC Model Bulletin, PCAOB AS 2201 amendments.

### Mission statement

To establish open-source, defensible governance patterns for autonomous-agent deployments in regulated US financial services — patterns that adopters can point at when their counsel asks "what's the audit trail for this AI decision?" and that regulators can read as evidence of operator-side discipline.

### Target users

| Audience | Use |
|---|---|
| FSI engineering teams deploying autonomous agents | Reference patterns + drop-in implementations for their governance stack |
| FSI compliance officers + general counsel | Regulatory-mapping documents (one per regime: SR 11-7, FCRA, ECOA, BSA/AML, etc.) to point internal audit at |
| Internal audit + model risk management teams | The hash-chain ledger, model inventory, and adverse-action gate as evidence sources during examination |
| Regulators and supervisors | Patterns library to compare adopter implementations against during examination |
| Academic researchers | A working reference framework for studies on regulated-AI governance |
| OSS-intake teams at Tier-1 buyers | A vendor file entry with documented governance, SOC 2 (post Item 5), and LF affiliation (post this application) |

### Governance model (proposed)

Technical Steering Committee of three members (author + two co-maintainers recruited per `MANUAL_REMEDIATION_AUTHOR.md` Item 7). Charter follows the LF AI & Data Foundation TSC template. Decision-making by lazy consensus + 3-day objection window for technical decisions; explicit majority for governance changes; super-majority for charter amendments. Public quarterly TSC meeting minutes. Conflict-of-interest disclosure file maintained per member. Code-of-conduct enforcement role per `CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md`.

### Neutral home rationale

The framework's commercial-advisory adjacency (the author delivers paid advisory engagements around framework adoption — see `docs/ETHICS_WALL.md` for the information-barrier posture) creates a structural concern for some Tier-1 adopters: a single-author project where the author also sells advisory services around the project's adoption. Donation to the LF AI & Data Foundation neutralizes this concern by (a) placing the project under foundation governance with a TSC that includes non-author members; (b) creating a public, foundation-stewarded space where adopters' governance contributions feed back into the framework without flowing through a commercial counterparty; (c) providing a sustainable home if the author transitions out of active maintenance. The neutral home solves bus-factor + advisory-adjacency concern + adopter contribution incentive in one move.

## Risks and tradeoffs

**R1. Loss of unilateral control over project direction.** Post-donation, the TSC + LF governance constrains the author's ability to set technical direction unilaterally. This is the right tradeoff for a project that is becoming a regulator-side governance reference; it is the wrong tradeoff if the author intends to keep the framework as a captive lead-gen surface for the advisory practice. Author's standing posture: the advisory practice is downstream of the framework's credibility, and foundation stewardship strengthens that credibility — net positive.

**R2. Trademark license vs trademark assignment.** Standard LF AI & Data Foundation donation includes a perpetual trademark license to the Foundation for the project's use, NOT an assignment of the trademark estate to the Foundation. The author's IP-holding entity (NTCI Consulting, LLC per Item 2) retains ownership of the "Autonomy Ladder™" mark per `docs/TRADEMARK.md`. This is the right structure — donation of trademark assignment is unusual and unwarranted given the cross-vertical use beyond this project.

**R3. CLA / DCO transition.** Existing contributor history pre-donation is governed by the GitHub-default inbound-license-equals-outbound-license posture. Post-donation, new contributions require DCO sign-off. The transition is one-way; the author should confirm with counsel that existing contributions do not require retroactive re-license.

**R4. TSC vetting timeline.** Donation cannot proceed without TSC formation per Item 7. If co-maintainer recruitment lags, the application slips. Mitigation: start recruitment immediately on Item 7 publication.

**R5. Advisory-adjacency disclosure.** The author's advisory practice around framework adoption (per `docs/ETHICS_WALL.md`) is disclosed in the application's "Neutral home rationale" section. The Foundation may have follow-on questions about how the advisory practice continues post-donation. Author's posture: the advisory practice continues under NTCI Consulting, LLC; the framework's foundation stewardship adds a credible neutral home that strengthens (not weakens) the advisory practice's defensibility.
