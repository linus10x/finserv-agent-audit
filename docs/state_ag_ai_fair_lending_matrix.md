# State AG AI Fair-Lending Enforcement Matrix

This document is a 50-state matrix cataloguing state-attorney-general-led
AI fair-lending enforcement and AI-targeted state laws applicable to
financial-services institutions. It exists because, under the federal
recalibration documented in
[`docs/cfpb_ai_lending_supervisory_landscape.md`](cfpb_ai_lending_supervisory_landscape.md),
state AGs are now the primary forward enforcement path for AI-driven
fair-lending exposure — and banks operating multi-state need a single
reference for the state-by-state posture.

> **Disclaimer:** Reference pattern, not legal advice. Regulatory
> characterizations are summaries; engage qualified counsel for your
> specific compliance determination. See repo-root
> [`DISCLAIMER.md`](../DISCLAIMER.md).

---

## Purpose

The federal CFPB final rule of May 2026 narrowed the federal
disparate-impact theory under ECOA. The federal rule **does not alter
state-law enforcement authority**, and state AGs continue to operate
under state unfair-and-deceptive-practices statutes, state civil-rights
statutes, and an increasing body of AI-targeted state laws. The result:

1. **State AGs lead.** With federal enforcement narrowed, the
   Massachusetts AG's July 2025 settlement with an online student-loan
   lender for AI-underwriting disparate impact is the leading state-AG
   AI-fair-lending case of record. More are coming.
2. **State AI laws stack.** Colorado, California, Texas, and New York
   have all enacted AI-targeted state laws with effective dates in
   2026-2027. Each creates state-AG enforcement authority, often layered
   on the state UDAP statute.
3. **Multi-state creditors need a 50-state view.** The bank's AI
   adverse-action pipeline must be defensible against the most-active
   state AG, not the median.

This document gives that 50-state view. It is updated to reflect the
posture as of May 28, 2026.

## Highest-Impact States — Full Section

### 1. Massachusetts — AG Campbell July 2025 settlement

The **first state-AG AI-fair-lending settlement of record**. AG Andrea
Joy Campbell announced on **July 10, 2025** a settlement with an online
student-loan lender whose AI underwriting model produced disparate
impact based on **race and immigration status**. The matter was brought
under Massachusetts chapter 93A (UDAP) and the state ECOA analogue.
The settlement included monetary relief, an injunction against the
specific model variables identified as proxies, and a forward
disparate-impact-testing commitment.

For multi-state lenders, the operative facts are: (a) chapter 93A
authorises treble damages and AG civil penalties; (b) the state ECOA
analogue authorises disparate-impact theories the May 2026 federal
recalibration narrowed; (c) the AG explicitly cited AI-underwriting
opacity and the absence of a documented fair-lending audit as
aggravating factors.

**Module coverage:** `equity_audit`, `protected_class_proxy_detector`,
`lda_search`, `audit_chain`, `adverse_action_gate`.

### 2. New York — NYDFS Part 500 + Oct 2024 + May 2026 industry letters

NYDFS is unique among state regulators in having both (a) direct
examination authority over banks, insurers, money transmitters,
mortgage bankers, and virtual-currency businesses, and (b) a published
AI-specific examination posture via the October 16, 2024 and May 21,
2026 industry letters. See [`docs/nydfs_part500_ai_mapping.md`](nydfs_part500_ai_mapping.md)
for the full Part 500 mapping. The AG holds concurrent UDAP and state
civil-rights authority that layers on the NYDFS examination program.

**Module coverage:** All v1.1-v1.3 modules apply; see the dedicated
NYDFS mapping.

### 3. California — SB 53 Transparency in Frontier AI Act

Effective **January 1, 2026**, SB 53 imposes transparency obligations
on developers and deployers of frontier AI models, with AG enforcement.
For FSI deployers using frontier models in any consumer-facing surface
(adverse-action, suitability, marketing, fraud-triage), the SB 53
transparency obligations stack on ECOA and on the state UDAP statute.

**Module coverage:** `model_inventory`, `vendor_score_gate`,
`autonomy_ladder` (the published A0→A4 classification provides the
transparency artifact), `audit_chain`.

### 4. Colorado — SB 189 (effective January 1, 2027)

The original SB 24-205 was **stayed on April 27, 2026** and largely
replaced by **SB 189, signed March 14, 2026**, with compliance deadline
**January 1, 2027**. SB 189 retains the algorithmic-discrimination
focus and AG enforcement authority but with revised scope and
notification obligations. FSI deployers should treat the 2027 deadline
as the operative date and plan their high-risk-AI compliance program
accordingly.

**Module coverage:** `equity_audit`, `protected_class_proxy_detector`,
`model_inventory`, `vendor_score_gate`, `audit_chain`, `autonomy_ladder`.

### 5. Texas — HB 149 (TRAIGA 2.0)

Signed **June 22, 2025**, effective **January 1, 2026**. TRAIGA 2.0
imposes high-risk-AI obligations on developers and deployers operating
in Texas, with state-AG enforcement. The Texas posture is the
business-friendly counter-position to Colorado's high-risk regime but
nonetheless creates examination authority and a baseline disclosure
obligation.

**Module coverage:** `model_inventory`, `audit_chain`, `autonomy_ladder`,
`equity_audit`.

### 6. New Jersey + Illinois — Disparate-impact-active under state civil-rights statutes

Neither state has an AI-targeted statute on the books, but both have
active disparate-impact enforcement under state civil-rights statutes
that the May 2026 federal recalibration explicitly does not affect.
Both AG offices have published guidance treating AI underwriting as
within scope of existing state disparate-impact authority.

**Module coverage:** `equity_audit`, `protected_class_proxy_detector`,
`lda_search`, `audit_chain`.

### 7. Federal cases setting state-AG-relevant precedent

- **Mobley v. Workday (N.D. Cal., class certification May 16, 2025).**
  Class certification on AI-driven employment-screening discrimination
  claims; the legal theory is portable to AI-driven credit decisioning
  and is cited by state AGs in fair-lending investigations.
- **Louis v. SafeRent Solutions (D. Mass., settled November 20, 2024).**
  Approximately USD 2.275 million class settlement, five-year score-use
  injunction on voucher-holder applicants. Demonstrates the
  state-AG-adjacent private-class-action enforcement channel. Already
  cited in the CRE companion repo's mapping; relevant here as cross-
  domain precedent for state-AG fair-lending theories.

## Operational Implications for Multi-State Lenders

1. **Build to the most-active state, not the median.** Massachusetts
   chapter 93A, NYDFS Part 500, California SB 53, and the
   Colorado / Texas / Illinois / New Jersey postures together define
   the operating envelope.
2. **Document the disparate-impact audit.** State AGs cite the
   absence of a documented fair-lending audit as an aggravating
   factor. `equity_audit` outputs preserved in `audit_chain` are the
   defensive artifact.
3. **Inventory frontier-model dependencies.** California SB 53 and
   NYDFS May 2026 letter both target frontier-AI use. `model_inventory`
   plus `vendor_score_gate` artifacts are the inventory + diligence
   artifact pair.
4. **Publish the A0→A4 classification.** California SB 53 transparency
   obligations are most cleanly satisfied by the `autonomy_ladder`
   published classification per decision class. This is also the
   board-reporting artifact under NYDFS § 500.4(d).
5. **Plan for the January 1, 2027 Colorado deadline.** Colorado SB 189
   is the next compliance-date forcing function. The repo's v1.2-v1.3
   modules close the technical control surface; the remaining work is
   programmatic.
